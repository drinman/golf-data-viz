# Adjacent Patterns

## What exists now

The strongest adjacent products do not merely report data. They turn data into:

- a public artifact
- a recurring recap
- a status signal
- a fast explanation
- a reason to come back

The products most relevant to Golf Data Viz are not identical in domain, but
they solve the same behavioral problem:
how do you turn a private performance log into something a person wants to see,
share, revisit, or compare?

### Useful adjacent product patterns

| Product | Core artifact or loop | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strava | shareable activities, progress visuals, challenges, year-in-sport | turns workouts into public proof and recurring status loops |
| Oura | shareable reports, AI interpretation, daily readiness framing | compresses dense data into a few understandable signals |
| WHOOP | weekly planning, recovery framing, behavior loops | creates recurring planning and review rituals |
| Spotify Wrapped | identity-heavy annual recap and social share package | proves people share when the artifact says something about who they are |
| Chess.com Game Review | immediate post-game AI analysis and mistakes summary | turns every session into a teachable artifact and return loop |
| Hevy and similar fitness apps | story-native workout cards and monthly recaps | show how mobile-friendly shareables can feel native instead of report-like |

## What’s actually working

### 1. The best artifacts answer identity, not just performance

Spotify Wrapped is the clearest example. People do not share Wrapped because it
is analytically perfect. They share it because it says something about who they
are. Strava activity sharing and yearly recaps do something similar in a more
athletic register: this is what I did, what kind of person I am, and how I stack
up over time.

For Golf Data Viz, this implies a major upgrade to the artifact itself. "Here is
my radar chart" is weaker than "Here is the kind of round/player I am right now."

### 2. AI works best when it shortens the distance from data to meaning

Oura Advisor and Chess.com Game Review are useful because they compress complex
signals into fast explanations. They do not force the user to parse every metric
before understanding what happened.

Golf Data Viz's AI narrative is moving in the right direction, but it is still a
component inside the result. The adjacent pattern suggests it should become part
of the artifact identity, not just supporting text.

### 3. Recaps create habit without requiring a daily social feed

Strava's year-in-sport, WHOOP's weekly planning and review cadence, and fitness
monthly recaps show that repeat-use loops do not require a feed. They require a
recurring moment where the product returns a new story.

That matters for Golf Data Viz because building a golf community feed would be a
huge detour. Recaps and comparisons are a much cleaner way to earn return usage.

### 4. Shareables win when they are channel-native

Strava overlays, Hevy cards, and Wrapped slides work because they fit how people
already share. They do not feel like someone exported a dashboard to an image.

Golf Data Viz's current share card is polished, but still reads more like a
report than a native story object.

### 5. The best loops create new curiosity after the first session

Chess.com Game Review does this extremely well. You finish a game, get a review,
see key mistakes, and want to play another game to improve the score. The
product does not need a broad feed to create repeat behavior; the artifact
itself sets up the next action.

That is the exact pattern Golf Data Viz should target.

## What’s been tried and why it stalls

### 1. Pure recap without identity becomes forgettable

Many health and fitness products can export data, but the artifacts that fail
usually stop at "here is your summary." Without a social hook, status signal, or
explanatory angle, the recap does not travel.

### 2. AI summaries become generic when they do not anchor to one sharp point

A weak AI summary paraphrases the obvious. A strong one names the pattern, gives
it a point of view, and creates a memorable framing device.

### 3. Public sharing breaks when privacy is too blunt

Health products show the limit here. People will share sleep or readiness only
when the product gives them control. Golf Data Viz is less sensitive than health,
but still needs explicit publication controls.

### 4. Gamification without meaning becomes noise

Badges, streaks, and ranks only work when they attach to a real behavior that
matters to the user. Empty gamification is easy to build and easy to ignore.

## Patterns worth stealing

- Turn each result into an identity-rich artifact
- Use AI to name the pattern, not just describe the chart
- Build weekly and monthly recap loops instead of a community feed
- Make share outputs feel native to stories, texts, and creator formats
- Give the user a score, label, or archetype that can improve over time
- Build compare loops around self, target, and peers

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- generic AI recap paragraphs with no memorable angle
- annual-only recap thinking when golf wants round-by-round and monthly cadence
- empty badges with no tie to meaningful improvement
- public-by-default sharing with weak privacy controls
- exporting dashboard screenshots and calling them artifacts

## Ideas this unlocks for Golf Data Viz

- An AI round archetype layered on top of the score:
  "The Scrambler," "The Wasted Round," "The Ball-Striker with No Finish," etc.
- A monthly or five-round recap that says how the golfer's identity is changing
- A target-handicap gap story: what separates this round from a 12-handicap version
- Channel-native share formats:
  - story card
  - coach card
  - group-chat text-plus-image
- A post-round "review score" or quality score that creates the urge to log the next round

## Raw findings

- Public artifacts work best when they say something about identity, not just output.
- AI is most valuable when it compresses interpretation and sharpens the story.
- Recaps and comparison loops are a cleaner retention path than building a general social feed.

## Implications for Golf Data Viz

- The core artifact should become more identity-rich and more channel-native.
- The AI narrative should evolve toward naming the round, not just summarizing it.
- Repeat-use should come from recaps and compare loops, not from more menus and dashboards.

## Ideas worth stealing

- identity-rich recap objects
- AI naming and scoring systems
- lightweight recurring recaps

## Ideas to reject

- generic AI summaries
- dashboard exports masquerading as shareables
- feed-building as the main retention strategy
